# Scene Targets

## Purpose

Define the main types of scenes the game should repeatedly build and stage.

This is not only an art document. It is also a mission-support document.

It helps answer:
- what kinds of spaces the game needs most
- how missions should visually feel different
- how environment, lighting, and character staging support gameplay beats

---

## 1. Briefing Scene

### Example Use
- boss meeting
- informant exchange
- pre-heist setup
- deal negotiation

### Environment Target
- private booth, office, back room, or reserved table
- warm local lighting
- clean composition
- dark background support

### Mood
- control
- suspicion
- hierarchy

### Visual Priority
- faces and posture
- table props kept minimal
- strong key light on main speaker

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- deliver mission information
- establish character power dynamics
- create tension before action

---

## 2. Contact / Pickup Scene

### Example Use
- meet a civilian ally
- get a key item
- receive coordinates
- observe city life before tension rises

### Environment Target
- market lane
- kiosk block
- open civilian street
- quieter public area

### Mood
- grounded
- transitional
- alive city rhythm

### Visual Priority
- one readable contact character
- strong environmental axis
- low-intensity movement around player

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- pacing control
- tutorial delivery
- contrast before combat sequence

---

## 3. Street Threat Scene

### Example Use
- gang encounter
- patrol escalation
- wanted-level pressure
- intimidation beat

### Environment Target
- narrow neon street
- kiosk alley
- wet commercial road
- signage-heavy urban corridor

### Mood
- danger
- pressure
- noir identity

### Visual Priority
- enemy silhouette readability
- reflective pavement
- red/blue/amber lighting contrast
- objective space framed clearly

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- introduce hostility
- support combat and pursuit
- reinforce faction identity

---

## 4. Warehouse / Combat Perimeter Scene

### Example Use
- gang cleanup
- raid setup
- evidence recovery
- first real firefight

### Environment Target
- warehouse exterior
- fenced perimeter
- industrial yard
- loading area

### Mood
- exposed danger
- tactical movement
- criminal operations in progress

### Visual Priority
- cover objects readable from a distance
- target area clear
- enemy positions understandable
- beacon or evidence marker visible after combat

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- core combat loop
- objective completion after clearing hostiles
- teaching the player to move from action to interaction

---

## 5. Port Intercept Scene

### Example Use
- smuggling interception
- cargo theft
- surveillance gone loud
- vehicle objective

### Environment Target
- container yard
- crane line
- loading dock
- open port road

### Mood
- industrial
- high-stakes
- visible and risky

### Visual Priority
- long sightlines
- large silhouette objects
- routes and lanes easy to understand

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- support larger mission scale
- enable vehicle moments
- enable route planning and multi-angle approaches

---

## 6. Quiet Aftermath Scene

### Example Use
- collect evidence
- secure scene
- debrief after combat
- narrative reflection moment

### Environment Target
- same mission area after threat removal
- dimmed action space
- reduced motion in background

### Mood
- relief
- consequence
- closure

### Visual Priority
- objective marker clarity
- reduced combat clutter
- player focus on a single destination

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- cool-down after firefight
- clear signal that mission is transitioning toward completion

---

## 7. District Identity Scene

### Example Use
- player enters a new city district
- mission intro pan
- chapter transition

### Environment Target
- strongest version of the district visual identity
- market axis, neon street, port skyline, etc.

### Mood
- discovery
- anticipation
- place recognition

### Visual Priority
- skyline read
- signage and architecture profile
- color identity of district

### Gameplay / Narrative Function
- orient player emotionally and geographically
- make cities feel distinct

---

## 8. Cinematic Staging Rules

For any important shot or playable intro frame:

### Use foreground / midground / background layers
This creates cinematic depth even with low-poly assets.

### Use one dominant light idea
Examples:
- lamp over booth
- neon signage in alley
- sunset axis in market
- crane shadow in port

### Use one dominant silhouette idea
Examples:
- seated boss in suit
- lone gang member in neon street
- officer against police lights
- civilian cart at market axis

### Avoid over-equal framing
Scenes become weak if every prop and character has equal importance.

---

## 9. Production Priority for Scenes

Build scene types in this order:

1. Street Threat Scene
2. Warehouse / Combat Perimeter Scene
3. Briefing Scene
4. Contact / Pickup Scene
5. Port Intercept Scene
6. Quiet Aftermath Scene
7. District Identity Scene

This order supports the core loop and promotional screenshots fastest.

---

## Final Rule

Every mission should consciously move through different scene targets, not only different coordinates on the map.

That is what makes the game feel directed instead of random.
